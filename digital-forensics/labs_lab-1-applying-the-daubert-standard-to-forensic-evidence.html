Lab 1: Applying the Daubert Standard to Forensic Evidence

Click here to Download Applying the Daubert Standard to Forensic Evidence (4e) - Maxwell King.pdf

This lab involved preparing digital evidence for use in a criminal investigation. The lab was set up around Beverly Gates, who was believed to be involved in a drug trafficking operation. My task was to act as a digital forensic investigator assisting the police department by analyzing a seized hard drive image. The main goal was not only to locate files of interest, but also to make sure the evidence was handled, documented, and verified correctly. This is where the Daubert standard connects to the lab because it focuses on whether forensic methods are reliable enough to be used in court.

In Section 1 Part 1, I reviewed the search warrant and filled out the chain of custody form. This section showed that forensic investigations do not only involve software. They also require documentation of who handled the evidence, where it was stored, and how it was protected. If this process is not followed, the evidence may not hold up in court, even if the technical work was done correctly.

The next part of the lab involved using FTK Imager to view the seized drive image and search for evidence. Some of the evidence was found in unallocated space, which was interesting because it showed that deleted files are not always gone. In the Windows NTFS file system, deleted files can remain on the drive until they are overwritten. I also looked at the Recycle Bin, which can contain evidence because Windows keeps information about the original file path and the deleted file itself using special file names.

After using FTK Imager to collect evidence, I calculated hash values for the files. I then used Paraben’s E3 and Autopsy to verify those same hash values. This helped show that the files had not been altered. This was one of the most important parts of the lab because it demonstrated how the integrity of digital evidence can be proven.

The most important lesson I took from this lab is that digital forensics has a legal side, not just a technical side. It is not enough to only find evidence. The evidence has to be collected properly, documented clearly, and verified with reliable methods. I also found it useful to see how multiple forensic tools can be used to confirm the same result. This lab helped me understand the importance of chain of custody, hash values, and repeatable processes in a real investigation.
