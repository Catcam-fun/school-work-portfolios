Lab 7: Conducting Forensic Investigations on Email and Chat Logs

Click here to Download Conducting Forensic Investigations on Email and Chat Logs (4e) - Maxwell King (1).pdf

This lab involved analyzing communication evidence from Outlook, Thunderbird, Slack, and Discord. The scenario again focused on Beverly Gates and possible criminal activity connected to the case. The difficulty was that email and chat databases can contain a lot of normal conversation, so being able to search, filter, and connect specific messages to the investigation was important.

In the first part, I searched Outlook email data using Paraben’s E3. I looked at email headers, date and time information, sender information, and an IP address linked to a sender. This section showed me that email evidence is not only found in the message body. The header can include technical details about the source, route, and timing of a message. This can help an investigator track where a message came from or connect it to a specific person.

The next part involved using E3’s sorting and content analysis tools to review attachments in the Outlook database. I checked graphic files and examined suspicious attachments with their original emails. This was helpful because an attachment by itself may not make sense, but the email can explain the context around it. I also found an email referring to the “Big Boss,” which showed how a term or nickname can become important when it is connected with other evidence.

The lab also included Thunderbird, Slack, and Discord evidence. In the Thunderbird section, I recorded information from an email header, including the email address, ProtonMail server, and IP address. In the Slack section, I examined workspace members, channels, and message contents. In the Discord section, I reviewed Beverly’s friends and conversations with other users, including additional evidence in the database.

I learned from this lab that communication evidence can reveal relationships, timing, planning, and intent. I was especially interested in email headers because they show details that are not part of the normal message view. I also realized that investigators should not look at messages or attachments by themselves. The context around the evidence helps explain why it matters. This lab showed me why email and chat databases are important in forensic investigations.
