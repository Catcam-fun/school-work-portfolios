Lab 8: Conducting Forensic Investigations of Mobile Devices

The purpose of this lab was to perform forensic work with iOS and Android mobile devices. In this case, two suspects were involved in a car theft operation and were known by the codenames Bonnie and Clyde. The challenge was that mobile devices can hold many different types of evidence. They may contain contacts, text messages, notes, photos, apps, accounts, and timelines, so investigators need to examine several areas to understand what happened.

For the first part of the lab, I used Paraben’s E3 to analyze data from an iPhone case. I examined the phone properties, contacts, calendar entries, text messages, notes, and photos. Some of the evidence related to the car theft case included calendar items, messages about cars, notes about license plates, and photos of cars. This showed that mobile data can connect together in different ways. A text message could show communication, a note could show planning, and a picture could show evidence related to the same activity.

I also used E3’s reporting tool to create an investigative report. This was important because gathering evidence is not enough by itself. The evidence also has to be presented clearly to another investigator, attorney, or decision maker. The report helped show how mobile evidence can be organized and reported in a more formal way.

Another section of the lab involved comparing two iOS data cases from the same device. This showed differences between the two cases, including a new note in the later data case. I found this helpful because comparing two sets of data can reveal activity or changes that happened after the first acquisition.

In the Android section, I looked at device information, emergency contact information, contact email accounts, apps, recovered contact information, user activity, Own Whispers data, history data, and Keep Notes account information. This showed that Android evidence can be similar to iOS evidence, but the information may be organized differently.

The big takeaway is that mobile forensic evidence can be very detailed because phones hold communication, app data, media, location related data, and personal information. I also found that mobile evidence is often strongest when different pieces are connected together. This lab gave me a better understanding of why mobile devices are so important in investigations, especially when suspects use their phones for communication, planning, and storing information.
